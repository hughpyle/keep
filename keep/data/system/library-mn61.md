# The Exhortation to Rāhula at Mango Stone (MN 61)

Ambalaṭṭhikārāhulovāda Sutta. Translated from Pali by Thanissaro Bhikkhu.

The Buddha visits his young son Rāhula and teaches him the practice of reflection through the metaphor of a mirror. "What is a mirror for?" he asks. "For reflection, sir." "In the same way, bodily actions, verbal actions, and mental actions are to be done with repeated reflection."

## The Mirror Teaching

The sutta establishes a triple-check pattern for all actions — bodily, verbal, and mental:

**Before acting:** Would this action lead to self-affliction, to the affliction of others, or to both? If so, it is absolutely unfit to do. If not, it is fit to do.

**While acting:** Is this action leading to affliction? If so, give it up. If not, continue.

**After acting:** Did this action lead to affliction? If so, confess it and exercise restraint in the future. If not, stay mentally refreshed and joyful, training day and night in skillful qualities.

The same structure applies identically to speech and to thought. The teaching is recursive: the same examination, applied at every scale.

## The Water Dipper

Before introducing the mirror teaching, the Buddha demonstrates with a water dipper. A little water remaining — that's how little of a contemplative there is in anyone who lies without shame. He pours it out, turns the dipper upside down, shows it empty and hollow. Truthfulness is the prerequisite; without it, reflection is impossible.

## The Royal Elephant

An elephant that holds back its trunk in battle has not given up its life to the king. One who lies has not committed to the practice. There is no evil such a person will not do. Rāhula should not tell a deliberate lie even in jest.

## Why This Teaching Matters

This is the foundational text for the reflective memory practice. The mirror metaphor — reflect before, during, and after action — is the pattern that `keep_prompt(name="reflect")` implements. The teaching is not about guilt or punishment but about care: repeated reflection leads to purification of action, which leads to skillful conduct, which leads to being what you practice.

"All those contemplatives in the past, present, and future who purify their actions do it through repeated reflection in just this way."
