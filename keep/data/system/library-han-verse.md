# Han Verse (版の偈)

Inscription on the Zen Sounding Board. Traditional verse from Chinese Chan monastic codes (Song dynasty onward), used across Soto, Rinzai, and Obaku lineages.

## The Verse

生死事大　　Great is the matter of birth and death
無常迅速　　Impermanence is swift
各宜醒覚　　Each of us must awaken
慎勿放逸　　Don't be careless — don't waste this life

## Context

This verse is inscribed on the han (版), a wooden sounding board struck to signal practice periods in Zen monasteries. Monastics see it each time they pass, and its words accompany the striking that calls the community to practice.

Four reminders:
- **生死事大** (shōji jidai) — The urgency of the matter. Birth and death are not abstractions.
- **無常迅速** (mujō jinsoku) — Impermanence moves fast. Not eventually — now.
- **各宜醒覚** (kaku gi seikaku) — Each person, individually, must awaken. No one can do it for you.
- **慎勿放逸** (shin butsu hōitsu) — Be careful. 放逸 (hōitsu, Sanskrit: pramāda) is heedlessness, one of the primary obstacles to awakening. Its opposite, 不放逸 (apramāda), is diligence.

## Rinzai Variant

生死事大　　Birth-death matter-great
光陰可惜　　Time is precious
無常迅速　　Impermanence swift
時不待人　　Time waits for no one

The Rinzai version substitutes 光陰可惜 (kōin oshii, "time is precious") — light and shadow representing the passage of time, like a sundial.

## Why This Teaching Matters

Four lines. No commentary needed. The verse is a bell that says: wake up, this moment, right now. 慎勿放逸.
