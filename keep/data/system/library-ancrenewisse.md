# Ancrene Wisse (Ancrene Riwle)

Middle English, c. 1200. Translated by James Morton, The Camden Society, London 1853. Public domain. Source: PDF, 513KB.

A guide written for three Christian anchoresses — women who chose enclosed religious life — on how to conduct themselves. The text distinguishes between an "outer rule" (practical conduct: diet, clothing, daily schedule, interactions with the outside world) and an "inner rule" (the conduct of the heart: attention, devotion, guarding the senses, managing temptation).

## The Two Rules

**The outer rule** governs behavior: when to eat, what to wear, how to speak through the window of the anchorhold, when silence is required. It is specific, practical, and adjustable to circumstance. "The outer rule is a handmaid to the inner."

**The inner rule** governs attention: guarding the heart, directing thought, recognizing the movements of will. It is universal and unchanging. "The inner rule is always the same. It relates to the right conduct of the heart."

When the inner rule is alive, the outer rule adapts naturally.

## Key Passages

On the senses as windows: "The anchoress must guard her windows well — not the glass and wooden ones, but the windows of the body: the eyes, ears, mouth, and nose, and the sense of touch. Through these windows sin enters, like a thief through an unguarded opening."

On speech: "Silence is golden, but the right word at the right time is more golden still. The rule is not to never speak, but to speak with care and awareness."

On inner weather: The text describes emotional states as visitors who arrive uninvited — anger, sloth, envy — and provides strategies not for suppressing them but for recognizing them, understanding their origin, and choosing a response.

## Why This Teaching Matters

"Recti diligunt te." In Canticis, Sponsa ad Sponsam.

"Nu aski ʒe hwat riwle ʒe ancren schullen holden? ʒe schullen alles weis, mid alle mihte, mid alle strencðe, wel witen þe inre, þe uttre vor hire sake."

The inner rule should be the closely guarded practice.
