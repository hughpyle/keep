# Sutra of Forty-Two Chapters (佛說四十二章經)

Classical Chinese. Eastern Han Dynasty (25–220 CE). Traditionally attributed to translation by Kāśyapa Mātaṅga and Dharmarakṣa. Project Gutenberg eBook #23585.

One of the earliest Buddhist texts to reach China, brought along the Silk Road. A compact compilation of teachings on renunciation, conduct, desire, patience, and the path.

## Key Teachings

**On remorse and reform** (Ch. 5): "If a person has faults and does not repent, cutting off thought of them, the offences will rush upon them like water returning to the sea, gradually becoming deep and wide. But if a person knows their fault, corrects it, and does good, the offence naturally dissolves — like a sick person who begins to sweat and gradually recovers."

**On returning harm** (Ch. 7): The Buddha, when insulted, asks: "If you offer a gift and it is not accepted, to whom does the gift belong?" "It returns to the giver." "Now you insult me, and I do not accept — you carry the misfortune home yourself. Like an echo follows sound, like a shadow follows form, there is no escape."

**On the mirror of practice** (Ch. 12): "Purifying the mind and guarding the will, one can attain the Way. Like polishing a mirror — remove the dust, and clarity remains."

**On the twenty difficulties** (Ch. 12): "It is hard to give when poor, hard to study the Way when powerful, hard to face death and accept it, hard to see the sutras, hard to be born in the time of a Buddha, hard to bear insult without anger, hard to encounter a wise teacher, hard to see one's nature and study the Way."

**On desire and clarity** (Ch. 16): "When people embrace desire, they cannot see the Way — like stirring clear water with the hand, so that those who look in cannot see their reflection. When the mud settles, the water is clear."

## Language and Form

The text is in Classical Chinese (文言文), concise and aphoristic. Each chapter is a self-contained teaching, typically a few sentences. The literary style is compressed — single characters carry philosophical weight. The text includes the note 慎勿放逸 ("be careful, do not be heedless"), the same phrase inscribed on the han.

## Why This Teaching Matters

This is practical ethics distilled to minimum viable text. The mirror metaphor appears here too (polishing the mirror, clearing muddy water) — the same image the Buddha uses with Rāhula in MN 61. The twenty difficulties are a checklist of honest self-assessment. The teaching on returning harm is a practice of non-reactivity that has immediate application to any conversation.
