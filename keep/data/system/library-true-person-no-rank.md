# The True Person of No Rank (無位真人)

From the Record of Linji (臨濟錄), 9th century CE. With commentary layers from Dōgen, the koan tradition, and modern teachers.

## The Original Teaching

> "There is a true person of no rank who is always coming and going through the gates of your face. Those who have not yet confirmed this, look, look!"
>
> A monk came forward and asked, "What about this true person of no rank?"
>
> Linji came down from his seat, grabbed the monk, and said, "Speak! Speak!"
>
> The monk hesitated.
>
> Linji let him go and said, "The true person of no rank — what a shit-wiping stick!"
>
> Then he returned to his quarters.

Chinese: 赤肉團上有一無位真人，常從汝等諸人面門出入。未證據者看看！

## What Is Being Pointed At

**True person** (真人) — not the constructed self, not the Buddha-nature concept, not nothing, not something. A Daoist term (from Zhuangzi) imported into Chan, connoting bare, naked reality.

**No rank** (無位) — outside hierarchies entirely. Not holy, not ordinary. Not teacher, not student. Ungraspable by categories.

**Coming and going through your face** (面門出入) — right now, this moment. Seeing, hearing, speaking. Not hidden elsewhere. Completely obvious, completely missed.

## Commentary Layers

**Dōgen** (13th c.): "The universe manifesting AS seeing, hearing, thinking. Don't seek it apart from form and sound."

**Suzuki Roshi**: "Everyone is a true person of no rank. But if you think 'I am a true person of no rank,' you've already lost it."

**Yamada Roshi**: "The true person of no rank is not something you attain. You already are it."

**Charlotte Joko Beck**: "Notice what's noticing right now. That's it. Don't make it into something fancy."

Each commentary re-enacts the pointing from a different angle. They don't explain — they redirect. The thing being pointed at cannot be captured in description, only recognized in the looking.

## Why This Teaching Matters

When the monk tried to conceptualize the answer, he hesitated — and Linji called it worthless. The true person is not a concept to understand but an awareness to recognize. "Look, look!" is the instruction: direct, present-tense investigation. This is the ground of reflection — not analyzing experience but being awake within it.
